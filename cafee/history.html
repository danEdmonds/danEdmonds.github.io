Our Early Days

  • Founded in 1989 with US DOE Funding with assistance from Senator Byrd
  • Examine the Impact of Alternative-fueled Engine and Vehicle Technology
  • 25 Years of Research, Development, and Innovation
  • $100 Million in Revenue with Limited State Support
  • Maintain Unbiased, Scientific-based Findings
    • Mix of Federal Government, State Government, and Industrial/Commercial Sponsors
    • Commitment to “Getting It Right”
  • Significant Field Work
    • Core Faculty and Staff, Graduate Students, Temporary Staff, and Undergraduate Students
1989
Designed, constructed, tested and deployed a Transportable Heavy-duty Vehicle Emissions Chassis Testing Laboratory. Transportable Lab was commissioned in 1990.
1998
Settling Heavy-duty Diesel Engine Manufacturers (HDDE) entered into Consent Decrees with the US Government owing to alternative ignition strategies.
2004
Settling HDDE study concluded after approximately 1550 tests incorporating over 170 different heavy-duty diesel engines spanning 1994 to 2003 model years. Post Consent Decree engines’ 30-second NTE brake specific NOx values were below the NTE allowance limit.
2005
A test plan was developed by USEPA, CARB and EMA to provide measurement allowances and development of the NTE Threshold.
Direct Final Rule by US EPA: “Revisions to In-Use Testing for Heavy-Duty Diesel Engines and Vehicles; Emissions Measurement and Instrumentation; Not-to-Exceed Emission Standards; and Technical Amendments for Off-Highway Engines.”
2010
CARB (September 7, 2010) and EPA (November 8, 2010) adopted particulate matter measurement allowances for conducting heavy duty diesel in-use compliance testing of 2007 and newer HDDEs (enforced PM in 2011).
2013
Published ICCT Study titled “In-Use Emissions Testing of Light-Duty Diesel Vehicles in the United States”
